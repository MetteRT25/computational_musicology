I will analyze seasonal playlists I made over the course of five years to analyze my taste in different seasons of the year. My seasonal playlists are summer and autumn with respectively 139 and 74 songs. The songs in the playlist are chosen by what my musical preference is during these seasons.

A summer song I will analyze in this portfolio is Relax, Take It Easy from MIKA. I chose to analyze this song, because I listen to MIKA’s songs a lot in summer. It has a tempo of 121 bpm, which is faster than the average tempo for the summer playlist (118 bpm). The valence of the song is 0.608. The average valence for the summer playlist is 0.492. This means that the song is “happier” than the average song in the summer playlist. It is due to the fact that there is a wide variety of songs in the playlist that I associate with summer.

A song from the autumn playlist is Umbrella from Duncan Laurence. This song has a tempo of 97 bpm. This is below the average for the playlist: 108 bpm. Thus, the autumn playlist has on average a slower tempo. The valence for this song is 0.08, which is really low. It means this song conveys more sadness than happiness. However, I do not think this song is a very sad song, although it is not happy either. The mean for valence in the autumn playlist is 0.288. This is lower than the summer playlist.

Below is a chromagram for a song in my autumn playlist. I used the euclidean norm to get an optimal visualization. It has mostly a C#, F and Ab. Other than these pitches, F# and Bb are also frequent. I therefore conclude the song has the chords F# and C#, which are considered major chords. This is similar to what I hear in the music. At the end, there is an instrumental outro in the song. The piano and guitar are the most present. In the chromagram, this outro is visible by a change in the pitches. Eb is played more, while the C# and F# are not played anymore.

A song from my summer playlist is Relax, Take It Easy from MIKA. Again, I used the euclidean norm. What immediately catches the eye, is the grey gap in the chromagram. This is a silent moment in the music. After it, you can hear someone talk. The talking has keys from all the pitches, while the music is very structured with frequent pitches such as F#, E and C#. This corresponds with the chords used in this song: F#m, Bm, E and C#m.

Here is my cepstrogram from Relax, Take It Easy. It looks for differences in timbre. As we already saw in the chromagram, there is a point of silence and after it a woman talking. We see these changes in the cepstrogram as well. For example in mfcc_02, there is a blue spot on 3/4 of the way. It occurs after the yellow lines. The blue spot is the silence. After the blue spot, you can see what happens to the cepstrogram when the woman is talking. There are no instruments you can hear when the woman is talking.

I made a self-similarity matrix from Relax, Take It Easy. The bright yellow means it is very different from the rest of the song. This is because there is a silence. So again, the silence part and talking part after it can be seen. Other than that, the structure of the song is:

  • verse 1
  • refrain
  • bridge
  • verse 2
  • refrain (2 times)
  • instrumental part
  • refrain (2 times)
  • bridge
  • outro

I think, especially towards the end of the song, the dark blue parts are the refrains. This means that the refrain is very similar with the rest of the song. The yellow stripes in between are the instrumental parts between the refrains. I think this, because the intro is also instrumental with the same melody and at the beginning of my self-similarity matrix you can see a bit more yellow.

This is my chordogram for the song Umbrella. You can see the blue parts are mostly around the chords Dbmaj and Bbmin. There is a yellow line at one point in the song. That means the chords are very different from the rest of the song. This is the case, because the song has a bridge. The bridge has different chords than the rest.
